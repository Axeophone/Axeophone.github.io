01 — Overview

Project Overview

For the PaceJam capstone launch, the team needed more than a working app — they needed marketing content that could communicate the product's emotional impact and value to an audience who hadn't touched it. I stepped into multiple production roles to make that happen: storyboard artist, director, cinematographer, composer, and editor.

The goal was to build a trailer that made someone feel the problem PaceJam solves — the frustration of running without the right music — and then the relief of finding it. Storytelling over feature lists.

My Role: Director, cinematographer, storyboard artist, editor (DaVinci Resolve), and original score composer (Reaper DAW). I coordinated with drone cinematographer Chris Gerlach for aerial coverage and with teammates Mel and Kylie throughout production.

02 — The Problem

What We Needed to Communicate

The capstone presentation required more than a product demo. PaceJam's core concept — music that adapts in real time to your running pace — is intuitive once you experience it, but hard to convey in a slide deck or a static image. We needed something that could:

  • Establish the problem emotionally, not just analytically
  • Show the app's value without requiring the audience to use it
  • Match the energy and personality of the brand Mel and Kylie had built
  • Work as a standalone piece — shareable, memorable, professional

03 — Pre-Production

Pre-Production

Storyboarding

Before any camera was picked up, I created a detailed storyboard mapping out narrative structure, shot composition, and emotional pacing — beat by beat. This wasn't just planning for planning's sake. The storyboard prevented wasted production time by ensuring every team member and collaborator knew the creative vision before we arrived on set. It also gave Mel the structure she needed to build a shot list, which drove production coordination.

Beta Trailer: Proof of Concept

Following software development principles, we produced a rough cut using stock footage before committing full production resources. This let us test the narrative structure and pacing, and gave me a low-stakes opportunity to get comfortable with DaVinci Resolve's editing environment. It also helped us identify what the story needed before we went out and shot it — saving the team from discovering problems on set.

04 — Production

Production

Mel created a comprehensive shot list from the storyboard, which drove our production coordination. Kylie served as the primary on-camera talent and runner. I handled ground-level cinematography; Chris Gerlach contributed drone coverage for aerial perspectives that added scope to the piece.

Production Challenge: Color Mismatch

The drone footage returned noticeably desaturated compared to the DSLR material — a common problem when mixing footage from different camera systems with different color science. This created a visual inconsistency that would have been jarring in the final cut and required significant color correction work in post-production to bring both sources into a unified look.

Lesson learned: When coordinating footage across multiple camera systems, align on color profiles and shooting parameters before production, not after. A brief camera-matching test on location would have reduced post-production work significantly.

05 — Post-Production

Post-Production

Color Grading

The bulk of the color work addressed the drone/DSLR mismatch. I used DaVinci Resolve's color correction tools to grade both sources toward a unified palette — high-contrast, saturated, and energetic, consistent with PaceJam's electric brand aesthetic. DaVinci's node-based color workflow was new to me, but its precision made matching sources across cameras genuinely achievable.

Motion Tracking & Dynamic Text

I implemented motion tracking and dynamic text placement — techniques I understood conceptually but had never executed under deadline pressure. Working through these in DaVinci Resolve while the clock ran was some of the most effective technical learning I've done. Deadline-driven skill acquisition has a way of making things stick.

Original Score Composition

I composed an original score in Reaper DAW using VST instruments and live saxophone, designed to mirror the emotional arc of the trailer — the frustrated runner before discovering PaceJam, and the energy of the resolution. The group ultimately selected stock music instead. It was a collaborative decision I didn't fully agree with, but learning to advocate for creative work and then accept the group's call is part of making media professionally.

06 — Reflection

Results & Reflection

The finished trailer successfully communicated PaceJam's value proposition through storytelling rather than feature description — which was the goal from the start. Audiences understood the problem and the solution without needing to touch the app.

The production gave me hands-on experience in areas I'd only approached theoretically: color grading mixed-source footage, motion tracking in DaVinci Resolve, and scoring to picture. Each of those was a new skill acquired under real deadline pressure, which is the most effective kind of learning.

Key Takeaways

Storyboarding prevents wasted production time

The planning document kept everyone aligned and eliminated surprises on set.

Match camera color profiles before shooting

Aligning drone and DSLR settings upfront would have saved hours of color work.

Align audio direction early

Establish music tone with the group before composing — saves creative energy on both sides.
